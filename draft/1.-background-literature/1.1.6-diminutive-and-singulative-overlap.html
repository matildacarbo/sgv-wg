1.1.6 Diminutive and singulative overlap

In this section, I discuss the overlap between diminutives and singulatives. I begin by going over the definition of diminutive, in terms of denotation and connotation.

I walk through earlier reports of the singulative/diminutive overlap. I address the semantic evolution from diminutive to singulative, then conclude with the relevance of the overlap to West Germanic.

Diminutives refer to something small. This is the core denotation of the category, and often mentioned as the main definitional aspect of diminutives.

For instance, Jurafsky (1996: p. 543) defines diminutives as "any morphological device which means at least 'small'", and Nieuwenhuis (1985: 1) states that "smallness is indeed the first meaning that springs to mind when one thinks of diminutive".

However, it would be a mistake to reduce diminutives to this single meaning. Diminutives frequently carry a range of connotations that may be diverse, contradictory, and making the denotation 'small' lose in salience. These connotations might express the attitude of the speaker towards a referent, or some other aspect of the speech act.

For example, pragmatic connotations of diminutives identified by Galeas (2004: 6-7) are:

  • ludic (non-serious, playful tone);
  • meiosis (polite attenuation);
  • baby talk (speech situation centered around a target of affection -- child, lover, pet);
  • emotive (expressing affection and various emotions);
  • familiarity and intimacy;
  • sympathy and empathy.

The pragmatic load of diminutives is significant, considering how common it is for multiple connotations to apply in a single utterance.

Galeas does not give linguistic examples for these functions, but bases his claims on pre-existing crosslinguistic literature (on Viennese German and Italian), and new data from Modern Greek (Galeas 2004: 4). Decontextualized sentences can hardly illustrate claims related to pragmatics, so linguistic data supporting these claims would need to be situated in an explicit context of utterance.

In addition to the previous connotations, diminutives can also carry a pejorative meaning -- expressing contempt, or disregard.

language base singulative
(X) Spanish papel
'paper'		 papel-illo
'piece of paper; unimportant paper'

(Adapted from Nurmio et al forth.)

Thus, diminutives tend to carry a wide set of connotations emerging from pragmatic uses, and they cannot be simply defined as meaning 'small'.

On the other hand, if a word carries some of the connotations outlined above without ever having the denotation 'small', there is no ground to call it a diminutive anymore. It should receive a different label (such as: hypocoristic, meliorative, pejorative, baby talk,...).

Nieuwenhuis (1985: 2) captures this nuance between denotation and connotation in his definition, which I adopt in this work:

  >"The term diminutive is used to refer to any system whereby the form of a word or words is changed for the purpose of referring to smallness, usually associated with an expression of endearment or contempt."

I turn to the matter of overlap in the expression of singulative, and diminutive.

The fact that this overlap exists is nothing new, as it was already observed by Jurafsky (1996: 555) and reported in German and Kambaata (Kagan & Nurmio 2024: 67; Wiltschko 2006: 1-2; Treis 2012: 7). Acquaviva (2016: 1177)'s overview of singulatives also mentions it, in the context of Welsh. Each of these sources gives one or more example illustrating overlap between singulative, and diminutive

(Adapted from: Jurafsky 1996: 555; Wiltschko 2006: 1-2; Treis 2012: 7; Acquaviva 2016: 1177)

The overlap between the expression of diminutive and singulative arises through the intermediate stage of expressing a part/whole relationship, such that the base refers to the whole, and the derived words refers to a part, and then a unit. (This is why some singulative word forms have occasionally been termed "partitive", as in Jurafsky 1996: 555.)

A priori, this relationship is unidirectional, going from a physical category (size) to a conceptual one (unit). This is in line with unidirectionality hypotheses developed to study semantic change (Jurafsky 1996: 540-544).

Note that the "singulatives" in singulative/diminutive overlaps involve singulative word forms, but not necessarily always true singulative morphemes (Nurmio, Dires & Carbo forth.; Kagan & Nurmio 2024). (See discussion of this point in 1.1.8 Typology > Distribution of overlapping diminutive/singulative.)

The overlap between diminutive and singulatives is relevant to my study, because this  overlap is reported in the literature for some West Germanic languages I study, such as in Dutch.

language base singulative
(X) Dutch bier
'beer'		 bier-tje
'glass of beer'

(Adapted from ([Broekhuis & Keizer 2012: 33](Broekhuis Keizer 2012 vol 1, p.33)))

Its existence is also supported by preliminary data I collected for an even greater number of languages in my set.

Thus, the starting point of my thesis, is that diminutives overlaps with singulatives in some West Germanic languages: more precisely, some suffixes that usually mean 'small' and/or carry various connotations, may be used to derive words that refer to units. Thus, these markers may or may not be true singulative markers, as my research elucidates.
