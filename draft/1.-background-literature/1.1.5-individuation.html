1.1.5 Individuation

Singulatives are not just sensitive to morphosyntax -- they must also be defined and investigated in terms of semantics. This is where a discussion of individuation enters the picture.

I discuss the scale of individuation (Grimm 2012), as well as natural units and packaged units.

The scale of individuation is an implicational scale proposed by Grimm 2012: 53-57, to describe cross-linguistic tendencies in number markedness. The higher a referent is on the scale, the more it is likely to be marked when singular, and non-singular when unmarked. (Non-singular might mean plural, collective, or general number, depending on the language's number system.)

Grimm (2012: 73) predicts that number markers usually target continuous portions of the scale of individuation. This is what makes the scale implicational.
More concretely, it would go against his prediction for a singulative to target liquids and insects, and "ignore" granular aggregates (which sit between the liquids and insects on the scale).

Let's now turn to natural and packaged units.
Natural units refer to things with fixed, stable boundaries, such as natural minimal parts (grains of sand, blades of grass) or individuals of a group or herd (a pig, an ant,...).
By contrast, packaged units refer to units made of substances without natural minimal parts. Consequently, the type of unit expressed must get conventionalized. Properties needed to count as unit = shape, size,...
(a cheese; a chocolate bar; a beer)

  • Some packaged units are closer to natural minimal parts than others (eg fruit jelly candy; a candy split in two may not be called a marmeladka)
  • Similarly, some natural units are closer to packaged units than others. Regarding vegetation, "unit of X" can in theory refer to multiple parts of plants (grain, seed, fruit,...). In practice, however, singulatives in this semantic class refer to a single unit (seed and not fruit, or vice versa) -- in other words, one unit gets conventionalized as the relevant natural unit for a given plant, out of a few possible options.

Let's bring everything together.
Referents of bases for singulatives are predicted to sit somewhere in the low-to-middle area of the individuation scale. The addition of a singulative marker to the base makes the word refer to an individuated referent.
In line with Grimm's typological predictions (2012: 73), I expect true singulative markers to target continuous portions of the individuation scale.
But what about markers that derive singulative word forms, but are not true singulatives: do they target continuous portions of the scale? This is unclear, and each possible answer leads to different implications.

  • Yes: ....?
  • No (as in: not necessarily): We can use non-respect of the scale as a feature of markers that are not true singulatives. Respect of the scale is a necessary, but not sufficient, condition to back the claim that a marker is a true singulative.
