1.1.3 Derivational and inflectional domains

Singulatives may fall in the domain of derivation, or inflection. I illustrate this with examples from languages in which it is respectively inflectional, derivational, and in a grey area between the two. In addition, I show some interaction between singulatives' domain, and grammatical number.

In Kambaata (< Afro-Asiatic) (4), the singular form of 'cat' and 'ficus' are marked, relative to the plural counterparts. The singulative forms are the canonical singular forms of some words, and they fall under number inflection.

On the other hand, in North Saami (< Uralic) (5), words with -čalbmi are not the singular form of the base they derive from, but rather distinct words that refer to units of the base. Singulatives formed with -čalbmi have singular and plural forms, which supports the idea that they are the result of derivation.

language collective singulative
(X) Kambaata adan-í-ta ‘cats' adan-ch-ú(-ta)[1] 'cat'
od-éeta ’(several) ficus’ od-ee-chch-ú-ta ‘(one) ficus’
(X) North Saami muohta ‘snow’ muohta-čalbmi ’snowflake (SG)’
muohta-čalmmi-t 'snowflakes (PL)'
suoldne ‘dew’ suoldne-čalbmi  'dewdrop (SG)'
suoldne-čalmmi-t 'dewdrops (PL)'

(Adapted from (X) Treis 2012: 3; (X) Ylikoski 2022: 1, 3, 7)

On the other hand, singulative Arabic (> Afro-Asiatic) -a(t) falls in a grey area between inflection and derivation.

When used as a singulative, -a(t) seems derivational, because adding it to a base does not create the singular counterpart of the base. The base is a grammatical singular referring to a plurality, and pluralized singulatives occur, much like in North Saami (Dali Mathieu 2021 Arabic: 24).

language collective singulative
(X) Classical Arabic: baqar 'cattle'   baqar-at (cattle-SGV) 'a head of cattle'
baqar-a-at (cattle-SGV-PL) 'heads of cattle'

(Adapted from Dali & Mathieu 2021: 24)

The blurring between the domains has to do with the fact that singulative -a(t) emerged through morphological reanalysis, and carries multiple grammatical functions, some derivational (such as nominalisation), and some inflectional (marking the feminine) (Dali Mathieu 2021 Arabic: 4, Fehri 2022: 134).

In summary, singulatives may fall under derivation or inflection. Surveying singulatives is any given language requires investigation in both domains. Studying singulatives' interaction with grammatical number assists in establishing which domain they belong to.

  1. The parentheses indicate an optional morpheme: without -ta, the form is masculine, and with -ta, it is feminine (Treis 2012: 3).↩︎
