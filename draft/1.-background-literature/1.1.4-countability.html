1.1.4 Countability

Next, I address countability and its relation to singulatives, beginning with a discussion of the terms: mass noun, non-count noun, count noun.

As explained by Grimm (2012: 3), "mass nouns" is an ambiguously used term. Depending on the author, it may refer to a grammatical category defined by its distribution (e.g. words that can be used with 'much', not 'many' in English), or a semantic group (mass-like referents such as substances ), irrespective of their grammatical behavior.

Like Grimm (2012: 3), I set aside the term 'mass noun' due to its ambiguity, and rather speak of count nouns, and non-count nouns. These terms group nouns according to their distributional properties, not their meaning.

The contrast can be illustrated by showing that some words are usually grammatical with "many", or numbers, and ungrammatical with "much"... whereas the opposite applies to another set of words. The first are described as count nouns, and the latter, non-count.

A superficial understanding of count and non-count nouns gives the impression that the two are binary, mutually exclusive categories, but closer study quickly reveals that this view is untenable.

There are several distributional tests for categorizing count, and non-count nouns, and, as Grimm (2012: 4) explains, " not all nouns which could be classified as countable or non-countable by some distributional properties behave identically with respect to all of the distributional properties. Another source of complication is that nouns often have several uses, some countable and some not."

With this view, nouns have more or less tendency to behave as count, or non-count, and a clear picture of their status emerges by testing them in different contexts (both grammatical and semantic). In the words of Grimm (2012: 11)  "countability is a matter of degree and [...] different nouns have different countability "preferences"."

Let's now turn to singulatives.

First, when singulative are described as selecting bases that are "mass", it must be understood in semantic terms. (See 1.1.5 Individuation for more on this topic.)

Second, singulatives interact with grammatical countability. A singulative word form largely behaves as a count noun, since it refers to an individuated referent.

In some languages, singulative markers target bases that tend to be non-count, and turns them into count nouns. A consistent shift from non-count to count indicates a 'true' singulative per Kagan & Nurmio (2024: 73)).

On the other hand, it is also possible for singulative markers to take bases that are count, and return a derived form that is also count.

However, a non-count word form, or marker that derives non-count forms, cannot be called singulatives, because the un-individuated meaning is incompatible with the individuating function of singulatives. Accordingly, if a marker occasionally derives non-count nouns, it is unlikely to be a true singulative, as in the case of Russian -k.

Thus, the morphosyntactic distribution of singulatives is sensitive to countability. Singulatives' bases can score high in non-count preference, or admit either count and non-count.

Additionally, singulative words forms must behave as count nouns by default -- meaning, in most contexts, with rare exceptions like universal grinding.

This information is useful for recognizing true singulatives, and is integrated in my investigation of West Germanic.
