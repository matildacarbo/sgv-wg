1.1.8 Typology

In this section, I discuss: the distribution of singulative markers across languages of the world, and the types of markers observed.

After the overview, I turn to the distribution of some categories that overlap with the singulative: diminutive, and grammatical gender.

Distribution across languages

Until recently, singulatives were claimed to be a rare feature, with a limited distribution in terms of geography and language families (Haspelmath & Karjus 2017: 1217).

For instance, Haspelmath & Karjus (2017: 1220) describe singulatives as "attested in substantial numbers only in some Celtic languages, in varieties of Arabic (such as Maltese), in Cushitic languages, and in a few other languages spoken in northeastern Africa".

Dali & Mathieu (2021: 18) discuss singulatives in Arabic and other varieties of Semitic (Biblical Hebrew, and Akkadian).

Grimm (2012: 22, 31, 36, 42) studies singulatives in four languages, all falling under one of the (sub)families reported in other publications: Welsh (Celtic), Turkana (Nilo-Saharan), Maltese (Semitic), Dagaare (Niger-Congo).

Acquaviva (2016) provides the widest coverage prior to Nurmio (2023). He states that singulatives are a prominent feature in Nilo-Saharan languages, Chadic languages (a branch of Afro-Asiatic, like Arabic), and the Brittonic branch of Celtic languages (Acquaviva 2016: 1173). He also reports singulatives in Russian, Dutch (Aquaviva 2016: 1172, 1175). The remainder of the languages with singulatives he presents are genetically unrelated and geographically distant -- Tariana (Arawak family; spoken in Amazonia, Brasil), Itelmen (Chukotko-Kamchatkan; Kamchatka peninsula, Russia), Burushaski (isolate; Pakistan), Swahili (Niger-Congo; much of East Africa) (Acquaviva 2016: 1173).

Nurmio (2023: 9; Nurmio, Dires, & Carbo (forth.)) challenges the idea that singulatives are rare, and aims to show that they are far more common than previously thought. What follows is a snapshot of the distribution of singulatives, informed by the unpublished database (Nurmio, Dires & Carbo forth.).

First, some precisions about the families most known to have singulatives are in order. Singulatives are seen in Brittonic and Goidelic branches of Celtic, albeit far more on the Brittonic side (Welsh, Breton, Cornish), than Goidelic (observed in Irish Gaelic; not yet in Scottish Gaelic or Manx (Nurmio 2023: 3, 13; Nurmio, Dires & Carbo (forth.)). They are observed in all subfamilies of Afro-Asiatic except Egyptian -- in other words, they are seen in Berber, Chadic, Cushitic, and Semitic.

Second, two geographic clusters emerge from the database. In both cases, the singulative may be an areal feature -- spread by contact across language families, in addition to transmission to daughter languages.

The first cluster centers in the Horn of Africa, with some extension to the North and West of Africa and Middle East. The list that follows presents language families in the area, which appear to have singulatives. (Note that the classification of languages of Africa into families is contentious; see Güldemann 2018. I rely on the classification given in Glottolog, as done in the database.)

  • Nilotic [1] (with representatives in all branches: Eastern Nilotic, such as Bari; Southern Nilotic, such as Kipsigis; Western Nilotic, such as Dinka);
  • Afro-Asiatic (all branches except Egyptian, hence: Semitic such as Arabic; Berber such as Nafusi Berber; Chadic such as Masa and Cushitic such as Oromo);
  • Maban (with Masalit and Runga);
  • Dajuic (with Shatt);
  • Kadugli-Krongo (with Krongo);
  • South Omotic (with Aari).

In this area, singulatives are highly variable, and mostly seen in the inflectional domain as expressions of singular number. More research on languages of Chad-Ethiopia (a tentative area for which a modest case is made in Güldemann 2010: 12-13) would be needed to establish whether we can really call singulatives an areal feature: this would require demonstration that they spread across families through contact.

The second cluster is diffused over Siberia, with some extension to Northern Europe (Lapland). We observe singulatives in:

  • Uralic (in half of the branches, which is to say: Mansic with Northern Mansi; Permian with Komi; Saami such as North Saami; Samoyedic such as Forest Enets);
  • Yeniseian (with Ket);
  • Chukotko-Kamchatkan (with Chukchi).

Singulatives in this area are less variable than what we observe in Africa, mostly falling in the derivational domain. They are colexified with terms meaning 'eye' in (at least) two language families. At this stage, it is unclear if there are singulatives in other families represented in Siberia (which is to say Tungusic, Eskimo-Aleut, Turkic, Nivkh, Yukaghiric) (Anderson 2006: 267). In any case, it is plausible that singulatives spread by contact across language families, through the long history of nomadism and small-scale multilingualism of Indigenous peoples of Siberia and Northern Europe (Khanina 2021).

Another interesting point emerging from the database is that singulatives appear to be a feature of Indo-European beyond Celtic: there are examples from Romance, Germanic, Slavic and Indo-Iranian.

Further research is needed to assess whether any of these findings involve singulative morphemes, rather than just isolated, unproductive singulative word forms. The present work contributes to clarifying the status of Germanic.

Finally, the database also reports singulatives in languages that are genetically and geographically disparate. These include:

  • Algic languages in North America (Fox);
  • Arawakan and Chibchan, two families in the Amazonian region of South America (Tariana,...);
  • Niger-Congo, especially the Bantu phylum, which covers much of the centre and south of Africa.
  • Sino-Tibetan in East Asia (Cantonese);
  • Austronesian in "Papunesia", which is to say South East Asia and Oceania (Nehan and others).

There is less known about the presence of singulatives in these areas. More research is needed to determine whether they are genuinely rare, or rather underreported.

At present, the database reports no singulatives in Australia, West Africa, Meso-America, or among signed languages.

Distribution of overlapping diminutive/singulative

I now address markers that carry diminutive and singulative meaning, touching on: types of markers, representative languages, and varying degrees of grammaticalization.

The distribution of marker type mirrors that of singulatives overall, with the same strong preponderance for suffixes.

In terms of geographic distribution, the overlap is mostly seen in languages from the two tentative areal clusters: "Chad-Ethiopia" (North/East Africa, Middle East); and Siberia/Lapland. We also observe the overlap in a handful of "isolated" languages (isolated in so far as the data collection says nothing about wider distribution yet): Ojibwe (Algic), Baule (Bantu), Kuna (Chibchan).

The overlap of singulative/diminutive includes diminutives with occasional, more or less lexicalized uses as singulatives; grammaticalized singulatives whose distinctiveness flies under the radar; and markers that fall somewhere in between these two extremes. In other words, it includes true diminutives with some singulative uses, and true singulatives. Thus, closer analysis of the data would dissolve some of this overlap at a synchronic level. In its stead, the relation between the singulative and diminutive would be absent, or historical, depending on the case. (See also 1.1.6 Diminutive and singulative overlap)

The unpublished database also illustrates its presence in Indo-European languages outside Celtic, such as in Italian. Our data does show that there are singulative word forms derived with a gendered suffix, Italian -ino. The suffix is derives masculine words, regardless of the gender of the base. Whether this suffix is a true singulative is currently unknown.

Pairing others than feminine/unit are also possible.

For example, Fox (Algic) has two grammatical genders, animate and inanimate (Goddard 2002). Fox has some word pair that alternate in gender, such that the inanimate noun expresses a mass or collectivity, and the animate noun expresses a unit.

In conclusion, recent research moves away from viewing singulatives as rare, and suggests that they are more common than previously thought, but still critically under-reported.

This thesis elucidates the status of singulatives in all West Germanic languages amenable to research, especially as it pertains to the diminutive/singulative and gender/singulative overlaps.
