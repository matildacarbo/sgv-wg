1.3 Singulatives in West Germanic

This section presents literature relevant to singulatives in West Germanic. It addresses whether there are any claims that singulatives exist in each language, and which marking strategies have (or might have) this function. I rely on grammars and academic articles. The academic articles I rely on generally concern number or derivation. Overall, the literature I consulted suggests that singulatives are expressed through diminutive suffixes, and variance in grammatical gender. There is no evidence of other strategies; I show that there are no singulatives in the inflectional domain, for example.

For all languages, I discuss whether diminutive morphemes might behave as singulatives. There are reports that they do in Dutch, Frisian, German and Yiddish, and hence they also might in closely related languages. This is nothing typologically unusual, as diminutives and singulatives overlap in several languages (Nurmio 2023: 6). I also address gender as a potential form of "singulative". There are words in Frisian that can refer to units when in common gender, and to masses when in neuter gender. Accordingly, I look for similar phenomena in other languages that have grammatical gender. This pattern is a less typical way unit/mass contrasts can be expressed through grammatical gender (whereas the most crosslinguistically common pattern seems to be that the feminine expresses units, as in Afro-Asiatic languages (Nurmio 2023: 14)).

Thus, the singulative strategies that I look for in my sample are: use of diminutives (11), and gender alternations (12).

Additionally, I investigated whether there might be singulatives in the inflectional domain according to current literature. I found no cases of marked singular forms derived from plurals in any targeted language. Rather, on nouns, plurals are marked through suffixes, and singular remains unmarked, as in (13) and (14).

Less commonly, nouns are unmarked for number, as in (15), or their plural marking is expressed through stem changes, as in (16). Similarly to nouns, agreement between nouns and dependent parts of speech (such as adjectives, articles) do not exhibit patterns where singular is marked, and plural unmarked: for instance, in German, singular and plural agreement are equally marked, and fused with gender. Gender categories get conflated in the plural.
